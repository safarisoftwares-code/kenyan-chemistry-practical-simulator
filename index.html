      Purple solution. Powerful oxidizing agent. Used in redox titrations.

      CO₃²⁻ — Carbonate
      Colourless. Reacts with acids to produce CO₂ gas.

      📐 How to Calculate Oxidation States

      📋 Rules

      1. Sum in neutral compound = 0
      2. Sum in ion = charge on ion
      3. Oxygen = -2 (except peroxides)
      4. Hydrogen = +1 (except metal hydrides)

      🧪 Example: MnO₄⁻

      Let Mn = x
      4×(-2) = -8
      x + (-8) = -1
      x = +7
      ✅ Mn = +7 (Manganate VII)

      🧪 Example: Cr₂O₇²⁻

      Let Cr = x
      2x + 7×(-2) = -2
      2x - 14 = -2
      2x = 12
      x = +6
      ✅ Cr = +6 (Dichromate VI)

      📊 Solubility Calculations

      Formula: Solubility = (Mass of solute ÷ Mass of water) × 100
      Mass of solute at given temp = (Solubility × Mass of water) ÷ 100

      📝 KCSE Exam-Style Questions

      1. Using the KNO₃ curve, determine the mass of KNO₃ that dissolves in 50g of water at 60°C.
        From curve: solubility at 60°C = 110g/100g water.
        Mass in 50g water = (110 × 50) ÷ 100 = 55g of KNO₃
      2. A saturated solution of KCl is cooled from 70°C to 30°C. What mass of KCl crystals will form if 200g of water was used?
        At 70°C: solubility = 49g/100g → mass dissolved = (49 × 200) ÷ 100 = 98g
        At 30°C: solubility = 37g/100g → mass dissolved = (37 × 200) ÷ 100 = 74g
        Mass crystallized = 98 - 74 = 24g of KCl
      3. Which of the five salts would be best separated from a mixture by fractional crystallization? Explain.
        KNO₃ and KCl — KNO₃ has a very steep curve (large solubility change with temperature) while KCl changes gradually. When a hot solution is cooled, KNO₃ crystallizes out first while most KCl remains dissolved.
      4. Explain why Ce₂(SO₄)₃ behaves differently from the other salts when heated.
        Ce₂(SO₄)₃ dissolves exothermically — it releases heat when dissolving. According to Le Chatelier's principle, increasing temperature shifts equilibrium to favor the solid (undissolved) form, so solubility DECREASES with temperature rise.
      5. A student dissolves 15g of K₂SO₄ in 100g of water at 40°C. Is the solution saturated? Explain.
        From curve: solubility of K₂SO₄ at 40°C = 15g/100g water.
        The student dissolved exactly 15g. The solution is just saturated — no more K₂SO₄ can dissolve at this temperature.
      💡 Key Note: The solubility of one salt does NOT interfere with the solubility of another salt. Each salt dissolves independently. This is why fractional crystallization works — different salts crystallize at different temperatures.

      White precipitate
      Magnesium (Mg²⁺)
      Mg²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → MgCO₃(s) ↓

      Zn²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → ZnCO₃(s) ↓
      White precipitate

      Fe²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → FeCO₃(s) ↓
      Greenish-grey precipitate
      Copper(II) (Cu²⁺)
      Cu²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → CuCO₃(s) ↓
      Blue-green precipitate
      Lead(II) (Pb²⁺)
      Pb²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → PbCO₃(s) ↓
      White precipitate

      🟠 Sulphates (SO₄²⁻)

      Calcium (Ca²⁺)
      Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → CaSO₄(s) ↓
      White precipitate (sparingly soluble)
      Barium (Ba²⁺)
      Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄(s) ↓
      White precipitate
      Insoluble in excess acid
      Lead(II) (Pb²⁺)
      Pb²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → PbSO₄(s) ↓

      Soluble: Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Zn²⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Cu²⁺ sulphates

      White precipitate
      Soluble in hot water
      Silver (Ag⁺)
      Ag⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl(s) ↓
      White precipitate
      Turns purple in sunlight

      Soluble: All other common chlorides

      📋 KCSE Solubility Rules Summary:
      ✅ Always soluble: Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺ salts | All nitrates (NO₃⁻) | Most chlorides (except Pb²⁺, Ag⁺)
      ❌ Common precipitates: Most carbonates | BaSO₄, PbSO₄, CaSO₄ | Most hydroxides (except Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺, Ba²⁺)
